# WS4.4 — Moment of Inertia Guided Notes

Purpose: Support Day 7–Day 9 lessons on Moments of Inertia.

1) Concept recap
- Definition: Moment of inertia (I) measures distribution of area (or mass) about an axis. Larger I → more resistant to rotation.
- Units and physical meaning.

2) Simple shapes (formulas)
- Rectangle about centroidal axis: I_x = (b h^3)/12
- Circle about centroidal axis: I = (π r^4)/4
- Thin rod about center: I = (1/12) m L^2 (note: for area analogs use base*height)

3) Parallel-axis theorem (reminder)
- I_about_new = I_centroid + A * d^2  (area version for 2D statics)
- When to use and common pitfalls.

4) Composite shapes method
- Break complex shape into simple components, compute each I about a common axis (use centroidal formula + parallel-axis translation when needed), sum contributions, and divide by total area if needed for centroid checks.

5) Worked examples
- Example 1: rectangle + semicircle composite — compute I about a given axis.
- Example 2: L-shaped section (two rectangles)

6) Practice problems
- HW4.4: simple shapes (rectangles, circles, axis shifts)
- HW4.5: complex shapes (composite area decomposition)
- HW4.6: more complex shapes (holes, non-standard composites)

7) Tips for students
- Keep units consistent; label axes clearly.
- Sketch component centroids and distances before calculating.
- Use symmetry to simplify; double-check signs for offsets.
